# Chapter 1　First Arrival: Changi Airport and First Impressions

---

## I. Departure and Arrival

In January of this year, I had the opportunity to attend the AAAI international conference in Singapore. This is an AI conference with a history of several decades. For a long time it was not considered the most prestigious academic platform in computer science, but as artificial intelligence has risen rapidly in recent years, the weight of this conference has grown accordingly.

My journey began on the evening of January 17th, departing from John F. Kennedy Airport in New York, with a layover in Abu Dhabi, then continuing on to Singapore. After more than ten hours in transit, I finally arrived at my destination on the morning of January 19th.

## II. Changi Airport: The First Lesson in Efficiency

The plane landed at Changi Airport. What impressed me most was how seamless the immigration process was. I passed through the airport's APEC lane — the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation organization provides a dedicated fast-track card for business travelers from member economies, allowing cardholders to bypass the regular immigration counters — I simply scanned my passport on the reader, a process that took about ten seconds, and I was through. Compared to the lengthy and cumbersome entry procedures in many other countries, this efficiency came as quite a surprise. The flight landed at 9:30 in the morning, and by 10 o'clock we had already walked out of the terminal.

This was perhaps Singapore's first lesson to me — efficiency is not a slogan here, but an institutional habit embedded in the daily operations of the city.

## III. Into the City: Uncle and the City's Contours

After walking out of the terminal, we called a Grab on our phones. This is Singapore's most widely used ride-hailing service, and before long a driver accepted our trip — a Chinese driver.

In Singapore, I first encountered a rather interesting form of address: if the driver is a Chinese man, people typically call him "Uncle"; if a woman, "Auntie." This form of address carries a warm, familial closeness that makes you smile. Later I learned, however, that this custom circulates mainly within the Chinese community and is generally not used when addressing Malay or Indian drivers.

It was a Sunday morning and the roads were unusually quiet. Changi Airport sits at the eastern tip of Singapore Island, and our car traveled along the coastal expressway toward the city center with almost no delays. Along the way, Uncle the driver enthusiastically pointed out the sights outside the window: that emerging commercial district, that financial center rising skyward, and those large-scale shopping complexes all known as "Malls."

About twenty-five minutes later, we entered the core coastal urban area of Singapore.

## IV. Landmarks: A City's Self-Expression

The first thing to catch the eye was a massive circular structure — the Singapore Flyer. Its silhouette was especially striking in the morning light, like a giant clock suspended by the sea.

Slightly further away, three skyscrapers stood side by side, their tops spanned by an arc-shaped structure resembling the hull of a ship. This was the world-famous Marina Bay Sands — one of only two legal casinos in Singapore is housed within it. Its very existence is a thought-provoking policy choice: this country, known for its strict laws and discipline, has placed two casinos at the heart of its city skyline.

Further still, a broad coastal park stretched out toward the sea.

## V. Andaz Hotel: The Vertical Logic of the City

The car left the waterfront and drove into the heart of the city, winding through a few streets before stopping at the entrance of a tall building. This was the Andaz Hotel, where we would be staying.

The hotel is not a standalone building but is embedded within a large mixed-use development. After entering the building, the elevator goes directly from the entrance level up to the twenty-fifth floor — that is where the hotel lobby is. In other words, everything below the twenty-fifth floor consists of commercial spaces, offices, and other uses; only above the twenty-fifth floor does the hotel begin.

This kind of vertically stacked use of space is not unusual in Singapore, where land is at an extreme premium. It reflects the city's consistent response to land constraints: grow upward.

## VI. An Unresolved Question

We stayed in Singapore for ten days in total. Most of the time was naturally spent attending the conference and handling work matters, but since the hotel was not far from the city center, each morning and evening I could still carve out scattered moments to wander through the city.

In these moments of solitude, I kept turning over the same question in my mind:

Why did a vast country like China, in the early years of its reform and opening up, choose to learn from a tiny nation like Singapore?

This brought to mind a widely circulated story. It is said that during Lee Kuan Yew's first visit to Beijing, Deng Xiaoping asked him: can China become a developed country?

Lee Kuan Yew later recalled that the people who remained in Beijing were China's elite, while the Chinese who had crossed the seas to Singapore were mostly laborers from the lowest rungs of society. If these immigrants of humble origins could build a modern nation on a small island with virtually no natural resources, then China certainly had reason to achieve modernization as well.

In this sense, Singapore is not merely a city-state — it is more like an ongoing experiment: how can a small country find its own way to survive in a world dominated by great powers?
