# Chapter 10　The Engineering of Labor: Immigration, Workers, and the Pass System

---

## I. Two Worlds, One City

During my days in Singapore, one phenomenon never left my mind.

On the streets, at construction sites, and across all kinds of building projects, I saw large numbers of laborers from Bangladesh and India — dressed in identical workwear, moving through every corner of the city in groups. In conference and professional settings, I encountered a very different kind of person: semiconductor engineers from mainland China, working for globally renowned chip companies, with comfortable incomes and stable lives.

These two groups share the same city, yet operate on tracks that are almost entirely parallel, with virtually no point of intersection.

This is not by accident. It is the result of deliberate institutional design.

## II. The Three-Tier Pass System

Singapore's management of foreign labor rests on a sophisticated work pass system, divided into roughly three tiers.

**Tier One: Work Permit**

This pass is primarily for workers in physical labor — construction, cleaning, shipyards. Monthly salaries typically range from S$900 to S$1,500. Housing is provided by the employer, family members cannot accompany the worker, and there is almost no institutional pathway for upward mobility.

The reality is often even harsher — many workers arrive already burdened by large debts from recruitment agencies, and face long working hours and limited protection.

**Tier Two: S Pass**

This tier targets mid-skilled workers, such as technicians and operators. Monthly salaries range from around S$3,000 to S$5,000. Workers may rent their own accommodation, but numbers are capped by sector quotas.

**Tier Three: Employment Pass**

This is the pass category for high-end talent: engineers, researchers, and various professionals. Monthly salaries typically start above S$5,000, and top-tier positions can reach S$15,000 or more. Holders may bring family members and are eligible to apply for Permanent Resident (PR) status and eventually citizenship.

## III. Geylang: The System's Buffer Zone

In Singapore, one neighborhood comes up repeatedly in any discussion of foreign labor: Geylang.

This area is dense with cheap housing and well-connected by transport, and has developed a complete ecosystem around the daily needs of migrant workers. Every Sunday, when most construction sites shut down for the day off, large numbers of foreign workers congregate here, creating a remarkable flow of people. In Singapore's urban planning, Geylang plays the role of a "buffer zone" — spatially separating the living sphere of lower-tier labor from the city's elite districts, and maintaining the outward appearance of order and cleanliness.

## IV. The Logic of Stratified Housing

Foreign workers' housing arrangements correspond closely to their pass tier.

Work Permit holders typically live in employer-provided dormitories or cheap rentals, with cramped living conditions and limited autonomy. S Pass or Employment Pass holders can rent HDB public housing flats or private apartments, and are integrated into a more normal urban life.

Where you live is determined by the pass in your hand.

## V. The Distribution of Costs

In this system, employers and workers each bear different costs.

On the employer side, in addition to wages, they must cover workers' housing and basic healthcare, as well as a foreign worker levy paid to the government. This levy is designed both as a regulatory mechanism on hiring low-wage foreign labor and as a source of government revenue.

On the worker side, they must absorb the pre-departure recruitment agency fees and daily living expenses in Singapore. For lower-tier laborers, these two costs often constitute an enormous economic burden.

## VI. A Highly Engineered Labor System

Taken together, Singapore has built a highly engineered human resource system.

At the bottom, it centers on cost control — using the Work Permit mechanism to bring in cheap labor that keeps the city's basic infrastructure and services running. In the middle, it aims for structural balance — using S Pass quotas to regulate the supply of mid-tier technical workers. At the top, it is oriented toward talent attraction — using employment passes and immigration pathways as tools to draw professional talent from around the world into the domestic economy.

The efficient operation of this system rests on rigorous stratified management and precise institutional design. It allows Singapore to simultaneously sustain continuous urban infrastructure renewal and continuous development of high-end industries, even with severely limited land and population.

Yet the other side of this system is tens of thousands of low-tier laborers, living in this gleaming city in ways that are deeply at odds with its gleam. The efficiency of this city is not without cost — but who bears that cost, and who is able to see it, is itself a question worth asking.
